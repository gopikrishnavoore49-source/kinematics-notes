0. The Language of the Universe

Vectors: Numbers with Intention

The Beginner View: vector is just an arrow with a length and a direction. Like instructions: "Walk 10 paces North."

The Pro View: The universe doesn't deal in diagonals. To solve complex physics, we force every diagonal movement into a grid of horizontal ($X$) and vertical ($Y$). This is called finding the components. We use Trigonometry as the mathematical cleaver to chop a vector into its $X$ and $Y$ shadows.

Solving Numerical: Calculus Double Differentiation

From Image 4 (Right Page): Sometimes we have to take multiple derivatives to find acceleration from a position equation. Given $x = y^2 + 2y + 2$ and constant vertical velocity $v_y = 5$ m/s, we need to find $a_x$.

First Derivative (Velocity): Differentiate $x$ with respect to time $t$. We must use the chain rule on $y$:
$\frac{dx}{dt} = 2y \frac{dy}{dt} + 2 \frac{dy}{dt} + 0 \implies v_x = 2y v_y + 2 v_y$

Second Derivative (Acceleration): Differentiate again with respect to time $t$, using the product rule on $y v_y$:
$\frac{dv_x}{dt} = 2 \left[ y \frac{dv_y}{dt} + v_y \frac{dy}{dt} \right] + 2 \frac{dv_y}{dt} \implies a_x = 2 \left[ y a_y + v_y^2 \right] + 2 a_y$

Since $v_y = 5$ m/s is constant, $a_y = 0$. The equation simplifies beautifully:
$a_x = 2 \left[ 0 + v_y^2 \right] + 0 = 2 v_y^2 = 2(5^2) = 2(25) = \mathbf{50 \text{ m/s}^2}$.

The Dot Product ($\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}$)

If you want to know how much two vectors "agree" with each other, you take their dot product. The formula is $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = |A||B|\cos \theta$.

The Ultimate Trick: Because $\cos(90^\circ) = 0$, the dot product of two perpendicular vectors is always exactly zero. We will use this trick to bypass pages of geometry and solve problems in a single line of algebra.

1. The Standard Parabola

Before throwing things off towers or up ramps, we must master the flat ground. If you launch a particle at speed $u$ and angle $ \theta$ from the ground, the Independence of Axes tells us everything. The horizontal speed $u\cos \theta$ coasts forever. The vertical speed $u\sin \theta$ is fought by gravity.

The Sacred Triad (Time, Height, Range)

1. Time of Flight ($T$): How long until it hits the ground? Set total vertical displacement $S_y = 0$.

$$ 0 = (u\sin \theta)T - \frac{1}{2}gT^2 \implies T = \frac{2u\sin \theta}{g} $$

2. Maximum Height ($H$): At the peak of the arc, it stops moving upward ($v_y = 0$). Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2aS$:

$$ 0 = (u\sin \theta)^2 - 2gH \implies H = \frac{u^2\sin^2 \theta}{2g} $$

3. Range ($R$): How far does it go horizontally? Distance = horizontal speed $ imes$ total Time of Flight.

$$ R = (u\cos \theta) imes \left(\frac{2u\sin \theta}{g}\right) = \frac{u^2(2\sin \theta\cos \theta)}{g} = \frac{u^2\sin(2 \theta)}{g} $$

Pro Insight: To maximize Range on flat ground, you need $\sin(2 \theta) = 1$, which means $2 \theta = 90^\circ$, or $ \theta = 45^\circ$.

The golden rule of kinematics: Horizontal and vertical motions do not care about each other.

We build the velocity vectors using $v = u + at$. Note that initial vertical speed is 0.

$$ \vec{v_A} = u_1 \hat{i} - gt \hat{j} $$ $$ \vec{v_B} = -u_2 \hat{i} - gt \hat{j} $$

We force them to be perpendicular by setting their dot product to 0:

$$ \vec{v_A} \cdot \vec{v_B} = (u_1)(-u_2) + (-gt)(-gt) = 0 $$ $$ -u_1 u_2 + g^2 t^2 = 0 \implies at = \frac{\sqrt{u_1 u_2}}{g} $$

Perpendicular Displacements: Let's do the exact same trick but for the position vectors ($\vec{S} = \vec{u}t + \frac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2$).

First, write out the displacement vectors for both particles:

$$ \vec{S}_A = u_1 t\hat{i} - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\hat{j} $$ $$ \vec{S}_B = -u_2 t\hat{i} - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\hat{j} $$

For the displacement vectors to be perpendicular, their dot product must be perfectly zero ($\vec{S}_A \cdot \vec{S}_B = 0$):

$$ (u_1 t)(-u_2 t) + \left(-\frac{1}{2}gt^2\right)\left(-\frac{1}{2}gt^2\right) = 0 $$ $$ -u_1 u_2 t^2 + \frac{1}{4}g^2 t^4 = 0 $$

Since time $t > 0$, we can divide the entire equation by $t^2$ to simplify:

$$ -u_1 u_2 + \frac{1}{4}g^2 t^2 = 0 \implies \frac{1}{4}g^2 t^2 = u_1 u_2 $$ $$ t^2 = \frac{4u_1 u_2}{g^2} \implies \mathbf{t = \frac{2\sqrt{u_1 u_2}}{g}} $$

Notice how the time for perpendicular displacements is exactly twice the time for perpendicular velocities!

The $60^\circ$ Velocity Extension: What if we want the exact time when the velocity vectors $\vec{v}_A$ and $\vec{v}_B$ are at $60^\circ$ to each other? We use the full vector dot product formula: $\vec{v}_A \cdot \vec{v}_B = |\vec{v}_A||\vec{v}_B|\cos \theta$.

We know $\cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}$. The dot product of the components is $-u_1 u_2 + g^2 t^2$. The magnitudes are found using the Pythagorean theorem.

$$ -u_1 u_2 + g^2 t^2 = \left(\sqrt{u_1^2 + g^2 t^2}\right) \left(\sqrt{u_2^2 + g^2 t^2}\right) \cos(60^\circ) $$

Substitute $\cos(60^\circ) = 1/2$ and multiply both sides by 2 to clear the f\fraction:

$$ 2(g^2 t^2 - u_1 u_2) = \sqrt{u_1^2 + g^2 t^2} \sqrt{u_2^2 + g^2 t^2} $$

To eliminate the nasty square roots, we square both sides of the entire equation:

$$ 4(g^2 t^2 - u_1 u_2)^2 = (u_1^2 + g^2 t^2)(u_2^2 + g^2 t^2) $$

Now we expand both sides algebraically. Let's expand the left side (using $(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$) and FOIL the right side:

$$ 4(g^4 t^4 - 2u_1 u_2 g^2 t^2 + u_1^2 u_2^2) = u_1^2 u_2^2 + u_1^2 g^2 t^2 + u_2^2 g^2 t^2 + g^4 t^4 $$ $$ 4g^4 t^4 - 8u_1 u_2 g^2 t^2 + 4u_1^2 u_2^2 = g^4 t^4 + g^2 t^2(u_1^2 + u_2^2) + u_1^2 u_2^2 $$

Finally, we group all terms to the left side to form a standard quadratic equation in terms of $t^2$:

$$ 3g^4 (t^2)^2 - g^2(u_1^2 + u_2^2 + 8u_1 u_2)(t^2) + 3u_1^2 u_2^2 = 0 $$

This is a perfectly solvable quadratic equation $x^2 + Bx + C = 0$ where $x = t^2$. Using the quadratic formula, you can find $t^2$, and taking the square root gives the exact time $t$ when the vectors hit that precise $60^\circ$ angle!

3. Playing with Signs

From your notes (Image 2): "If displacement upwards is +ve, then displacement downwards is -ve." Sign conventions are your best friend. Physics blindly obeys your frame of reference.

Problem: ngled Projection from a Tower

If you fire a particle upwards from a tower at angle $ \theta$, why is the displacement $h$ negative?

  • Velocity ($u_y$): You aimed UP into the sky, so $u_y = +u\sin \theta$.
  • Gravity ($a_y$): Gravity LWYS pulls down, so $a_y = -g$.
  • Displacement ($S_y$): When it hits the ground, it is physically below your starting zero-line. Thus, $S_y = -h$.

Plugging these into $S_y = u_y at + \frac{1}{2}a_y t^2$ gives:

$$ -h = (u\sin \theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 - (u\sin \theta)t $$

4. Tilting the World (Inclines)

From your notes (Images 3 & 4). When solving problems on an inclined plane at angle $\alpha$, do not force standard X/Y coordinates. Tilt your head. Make the X-axis run parallel up the ramp.

The Geometry Proof: Why does gravity tilt by exactly $\alpha$?

Your notes feature a precise triangle diagram to prove this. Let's walk through it:

  • Draw a right-angled triangle where the hypotenuse is the ramp. The base is the ground, and the angle between them is $\alpha$.
  • The gravity vector $\vec{g}$ points straight down, perpendicular to the base. This forms a smaller right-angled triangle with the ramp, where the top angle is $90^\circ - \alpha$.
  • Our new Y-axis is defined as perfectly perpendicular to the ramp (making a $90^\circ$ angle with it).
  • Therefore, the angle between the Y-axis and the gravity vector $\vec{g}$ must be $90^\circ - (90^\circ - \alpha) = \mathbf{\alpha}$.
  • Basic trigonometry now dictates the components: the side adjacent to the angle (pressing into the ramp) is $\mathbf{g\cos\alpha}$. The side opposite to the angle (pulling down the ramp) is $\mathbf{g\sin\alpha}$.

Time of Flight ($T$)

The particle lands back on the ramp when its perpendicular distance from the ramp becomes zero ($S_y = 0$). Let's set up the Y-axis equation of motion:

  • Initial Y-Velocity: $u_y = u\sin( \theta - \alpha)$
  • Y-Acceleration: $a_y = -g\cos\alpha$
$$ S_y = u_y T + \frac{1}{2}a_y T^2 $$ $$ 0 = [u\sin( \theta - \alpha)]T - \frac{1}{2}[g\cos\alpha]T^2 $$

Factor out $T$ (ignoring the $T=0$ launch case) and solve:

$$ \frac{1}{2}g\cos\alpha \cdot T = u\sin( \theta - \alpha) \implies \mathbf{T = \frac{2u\sin( \theta-\alpha)}{g\cos\alpha}} $$

Adjacent Concept: Range up the Incline ($R$)

How far up the ramp does it land? We must find the displacement along the X-axis ($S_x$) at the exact moment it lands ($t = T$). The algebra is famously brutal, but let's walk through the full proof from Image 5:

  • Initial X-Velocity: $u_x = u\cos( \theta - \alpha)$
  • X-Acceleration: $a_x = -g\sin\alpha$ (Gravity slows it down as it climbs)

Plug these into the X-axis equation of motion:

$$ R = [u\cos( \theta - \alpha)]T - \frac{1}{2}[g\sin\alpha]T^2 $$

Now, substitute the massive $T$ we just derived:

$$ R = u\cos( \theta - \alpha) \left[ \frac{2u\sin( \theta-\alpha)}{g\cos\alpha} \right] - \frac{1}{2}g\sin\alpha \left[ \frac{2u\sin( \theta-\alpha)}{g\cos\alpha} \right]^2 $$

Expand both terms. To subt\fract them, we need a common denominator of $g\cos^2\alpha$, so we multiply the first term's numerator and denominator by $\cos\alpha$:

$$ R = \frac{2u^2 \sin( \theta-\alpha)\cos( \theta-\alpha)\cos\alpha}{g\cos^2\alpha} - \frac{2u^2 \sin^2( \theta-\alpha)\sin\alpha}{g\cos^2\alpha} $$

Factor out the common $\frac{2u^2 \sin( \theta-\alpha)}{g\cos^2\alpha}$:

$$ R = \frac{2u^2 \sin( \theta-\alpha)}{g\cos^2\alpha} \Big[ \cos( \theta-\alpha)\cos\alpha - \sin( \theta-\alpha)\sin\alpha \Big] $$

Now apply the beautiful trigonometric identity: $\cos \cos B - \sin \sin B = \cos(+B)$. Here, $A = \theta-\alpha$ and $B = \alpha$. Thus, $+B = \theta-\alpha+\alpha = \theta$!

The entire bracket collapses elegantly into $\cos \theta$:

$$ \mathbf{R = \frac{2u^2 \sin( \theta-\alpha)\cos \theta}{g\cos^2\alpha}} $$

For a projectile to smash "normally" (perpendicularly) into the ramp, it must have stopped moving forward along the ramp at impact ($v_x = 0$). Setting $u_x + a_x T = 0$ yields:

$$ \cot\alpha = 2 \tan( \theta - \alpha) $$

5. The River and the Boat

From your notes (Image 5). Relative motion is vector addition. The river is a treadmill ($V_R$). Your boat has engine power ($V_{BR}$). Your true path over the water is the vector sum: $\vec{V_{Net}} = \vec{V_{BR}} + \vec{V_R}$.

The Shortest Path (Zero Drift)

To land exactly opposite, aim upstream to cancel the river: $V_{BR}\sin \theta = V_R$.

The Drift Intuition (From Notes): What if you aim poorly?

  • If $V_R > V_{BR}\sin \theta$: The river wins. You drift downstream (right).
  • If $V_R < V_{BR}\sin \theta$: Your engine wins. You drift upstream (left).
  • If $V_R = V_{BR}\sin \theta$: Perfect balance. Zero drift.

Crossing Time (Pythagorean Formula): The true velocity straight across the river forms the third leg of a right triangle. By Pythagoras, $V_{Net} = \sqrt{V_{BR}^2 - V_R^2}$. Time is distance over speed:

$$ at = \frac{d}{V_{BR} \cos \theta} = \frac{d}{\sqrt{V_{BR}^2 - V_R^2}} $$

Numeric Example (From Notes): If river $V_R = 5$ km/h and boat $V_{BR} = 10$ km/h. $\sin \theta = 5/10 = 1/2$. Therefore, $ \theta = 30^\circ$ upstream. Angle with the flow = $90^\circ + 30^\circ = 120^\circ$.

Calculus Deep Dive: The Unbeatable River

What if the river is faster than your boat ($V_R > V_{BR}$)? You can't achieve zero drift. You must minimize drift using calculus. We write the drift $x$ as a function of aiming angle $ \theta$:

$$ x = (\text{Net Horizontal Velocity}) imes \text{Time} $$ $$ x = (V_R - V_{BR}\sin \theta) imes \left( \frac{d}{V_{BR}\cos \theta} \right) = d \left[ \frac{V_R}{V_{BR}}\sec \theta - \tan \theta \right] $$

Take the derivative and set it to exactly zero ($\frac{dx}{d \theta} = 0$) to find the minimum:

$$ \frac{dx}{d \theta} = d \left[ \frac{V_R}{V_{BR}}\sec \theta\tan \theta - \sec^2 \theta \right]A = 0 $$ $$ \frac{V_R}{V_{BR}} \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos^2 \theta} = \frac{1}{\cos^2 \theta} \implies \sin \theta = \frac{V_{BR}}{V_R} $$

This proves you should let the river push you, aiming exactly at $\sin^{-1}(V_{BR}/V_R)$ to minimize total drift!

Chapter 1.5: The Equation of Trajectory

So far, we've used time ($t$) to track projectiles. But time is invisible. If you photograph a water fountain, you don't see time—you see a pure geometric shape: a Parabola. Let's eliminate time and derive the Equation of Trajectory.

We start with our horizontal and vertical position equations:

$$ x = (u\cos \theta)t \implies at = \f\frac{x}{u\cos \theta} $$

Now, substitute this $t$ into the vertical $y$ equation: $y = (u\sin \theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$

$$ y = x\tan \theta - \frac{g x^2}{2 u^2 \cos^2 \theta} $$

The Expert Interpretation: This equation is profoundly elegant. The first term ($x\tan \theta$) is a straight line aiming up at the launch angle. If there were no gravity, the object would travel endlessly along this line. The second term ($-\frac{g x^2}{2 u^2 \cos^2 \theta}$) is gravity pulling it down away from that straight line. parabola is simply a straight line being bent downwards by $x^2$.

1. The Master Range on Inclines

In Chapter 1, we calculated the Time of Flight on an incline. But how far does the projectile actually travel along the ramp? Your notes reveal a massive mathematical shortcut: The distance along the ramp is simply the horizontal ground distance divided by $\cos\alpha$.

Shooting UP the Incline

By applying the trig identity $2\sin \cos B = \sin(+B) + \sin(-B)$, your notes derive the maximum possible range when shooting up a ramp of angle $\alpha$:

$$ (R_{max})_{up} = \frac{u^2}{g(1 + \sin\alpha)} $$

Shooting DOWN the Incline

If you stand at the top of the hill and shoot downwards, gravity actually helps your horizontal speed. The formula mirrors the upward shot, but the sign flips:

$$ (R_{max})_{down} = \frac{u^2}{g(1 - \sin\alpha)} $$
View the Full Trigonometric Proof

From the kinematic equation $S_x = u_x T + \frac{1}{2} a_x T^2$ along the incline, the general range $R$ is:

$$ R = \frac{u^2}{g\cos^2\alpha} [ \sin(2 \theta - \alpha) - \sin\alpha ] $$

To maximize this range for a fixed speed $u$ and ramp $\alpha$, we must maximize the variable term $\sin(2 \theta - \alpha)$. The maximum value of any sine function is exactly $1$.

$$ R_{max} = \frac{u^2}{g\cos^2\alpha} [ 1 - \sin\alpha ] $$

We then use the Pythagorean identity $\cos^2\alpha = 1 - \sin^2\alpha$, which factors into $(1 - \sin\alpha)(1 + \sin\alpha)$:

$$ R_{max} = \frac{u^2(1 - \sin\alpha)}{g(1 - \sin\alpha)(1 + \sin\alpha)} $$

The $(1 - \sin\alpha)$ terms gracefully cancel out, leaving the final elegant formula for shooting UP the incline:

$$ (R_{max})_{up} = \frac{u^2}{g(1 + \sin\alpha)} $$

For shooting DOWN the incline, gravity assists rather than opposes the horizontal motion relative to the ramp. Mathematically, we substitute $-\alpha$ into the original general range equation. Since $\sin(-\alpha) = -\sin(\alpha)$, the final sign flips to $(1 - \sin\alpha)$ in the denominator.

The Harmonic Mean Discovery (Deep Dive)

Your notes contain a beautiful, elegant mathematical relationship. Let the maximum range UP the incline be $R_2$ and DOWN the incline be $R_1$. Let's add their inverses:

$$ \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} = \frac{g(1 - \sin\alpha)}{u^2} + \frac{g(1 + \sin\alpha)}{u^2} $$ $$ \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} = \frac{2g}{u^2} $$

But we know that the maximum range on a completely flat ground ($R_{flat}$) is $\frac{u^2}{g}$. Therefore:

$$ \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} = \frac{2}{R_{flat}} \implies R_{flat} = \frac{2 R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2} $$

Conclusion: The maximum range on flat ground is the exact Harmonic Mean of the maximum ranges up and down the incline. Physics is poetic.

Adjacent Concept: The Angle of Maximum Range

We know the maximum ranges ($R_{up}$ and $R_{down}$), but at what angle must you aim to achieve them?

On flat ground, you shoot at $45^\circ$ (which exactly bisects the $90^\circ$ angle between the ground and the vertical gravity). The same universal logic applies to inclines! To maximize range, you must bisect the angle between the inclined plane and the vertical.

  • Shooting UP: The angle between the incline and vertical is $90^\circ - \alpha$. Bisecting it gives a firing angle of $ \theta = 45^\circ + \frac{\alpha}{2}$ relative to the horizontal.
  • Shooting DOWN: The angle between the downward incline and vertical is $90^\circ + \alpha$. Bisecting it gives $ \theta = 45^\circ - \frac{\alpha}{2}$.

This symmetry is why the Harmonic Mean exists. The geometry of the universe is perfectly balanced.

2. Precision Targeting (Wind & Bird)

Crossing a river to the "opposite bank" is easy. But what if you need to hit a specific point (Point B) on the map while a crosswind (or current) is pushing you? Your notes outline the ultimate "Perpendicular Cancellation" rule.

The Rule of Targeting

To travel exactly along a straight line from to B, the components of your engine and the wind that are perpendicular to the line B must perfectly cancel each other out.

The Numeric Example

Set them equal: $4 \sin\alpha = 1.2 \implies \sin\alpha = 0.3$. The bird must aim slightly into the wind!

3. The Relativity of Rain

Have you ever noticed that when you run in the rain, the rain feels like it's hitting you in the face, even if it's falling straight down? This is purely relative motion.

Your notes state the core equation: $\vec{v}_{Rm} = \vec{v}_R - \vec{v}_m$.

This means the Relative Velocity of Rain with respect to Man ($\vec{v}_{Rm}$) is found by taking the true rain vector ($\vec{v}_R$) and subt\fracting the man's vector ($\vec{v}_m$).

The Vector Triangle

To subt\fract the man's vector, we just add it in reverse. The rain falls straight down ($\downarrow$). The man runs East ($\rightarrow$). So we add a "fake" wind blowing West ($\leftarrow$). The resultant vector is a diagonal slashing into his face.

$$ \tan \theta = \frac{V_{man}}{V_{rain}} $$

where $ \theta$ is the angle you must tilt your umbrella forward from the vertical.

Adjacent Concept: Wind-Blown Slanted Rain

What if there is already a strong wind blowing, so the rain is hitting the ground at an angle $\phi$ to the vertical?

The true rain vector is now diagonal. It has a vertical dropping speed $v_y$ and a horizontal wind speed $v_x$. If a man starts running, how fast must he run so the rain feels like it's falling perfectly straight down on his head?

1. Prerequisite: The Equation of Trajectory

Concept: Deriving the Trajectory Equation from Scratch

Before we solve any advanced problem, we need the master equation that describes the path (shape) of any projectile. This is called the Equation of Trajectory.

Setup: projectile is launched from the origin with speed $u$ at angle $ \theta$ above the horizontal. We want to find $y$ as a function of $x$ (eliminating time $t$).

Step 1 — Horizontal Motion: There is no acceleration horizontally ($a_x = 0$).
$x = u\cos \theta \cdot at \implies \boxed{t = \frac{x}{u\cos \theta}}$

Step 2 — Vertical Motion: Gravity pulls down with $a_y = -g$.
$y = u\sin \theta \cdot at - \frac{1}{2}g t^2$

Step 3 — Substitute $t$:
$y = u\sin \theta \cdot \frac{x}{u\cos \theta} - \frac{1}{2}g\left(\frac{x}{u\cos \theta}\right)^2$
$y = x\tan \theta - \frac{gx^2}{2u^2\cos^2 \theta}$

Step 4 — Elegant Form: Since Range $R = \frac{u^2 \sin 2 \theta}{g} = \frac{2u^2 \sin \theta \cos \theta}{g}$, we can write $\frac{g}{2u^2\cos^2 \theta} = \frac{\tan \theta}{R}$. Substituting:
$$\boxed{y = x\tan \theta\left(1 - \frac{x}{R}\right)}$$

This is a downward-opening parabola passing through $(0, 0)$ and $(R, 0)$. Its maximum is at $x = R/2$, giving $y_{max} = H = \frac{R\tan \theta}{4} = \frac{u^2\sin^2 \theta}{2g}$.

Why this matters: This single equation lets us check whether a projectile passes through any point $(x, y)$ in space — which is exactly what we need for the hexagon problem, the collision problems, and more.

Concept: Key Relationships — Range, Height, and Angle

From the trajectory equation, we can derive several powerful identities:

1. Range-Height Relation:
$\frac{H}{R} = \frac{u^2\sin^2 \theta / 2g}{u^2\sin 2 \theta / g} = \frac{\sin^2 \theta}{2 \cdot 2\sin \theta\cos \theta} = \frac{\tan \theta}{4}$
$$\boxed{\frac{H}{R} = \frac{\tan \theta}{4}}$$

2. Problem 33 (Pg 194): Given $R = 2h\tan\alpha$ and $H = h$:
$\frac{H}{R} = \frac{h}{2h\tan\alpha} = \frac{1}{2\tan\alpha}$. But we also know $\frac{H}{R} = \frac{\tan \theta}{4}$.
$\frac{\tan \theta}{4} = \frac{1}{2\tan\alpha} \implies \boxed{\tan \theta imes \tan\alpha = 2}$
This elegant result means if you know the angle of the inclined plane ($\alpha$), the launch angle ($ \theta$) is completely determined!

2. Velocity Decomposition & Vector Triangles

Concept: Breaking Velocity into Components (from Scratch)

Every velocity vector can be split into two perpendicular components. If a particle moves with speed $v$ at angle $ \theta$ to the horizontal:

$u_y = v\sin \theta$ (vertical component)

The magnitude is recovered by: $v = \sqrt{u_x^2 + u_y^2}$ and the angle by: $\tan \theta = \frac{u_y}{u_x}$.

Why this is fundamental: In projectile motion, horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent. Gravity only affects $u_y$. The horizontal component $u_x$ remains constant forever (no air resistance). This independence is the entire foundation of 2D kinematics.

3. Extending to 3D — Projectile Motion in Space

Concept: How 3D Projectile Motion Works (from Scratch)

In 2D, we had two axes: horizontal ($x$) and vertical ($y$). In 3D projectile motion, we add a third horizontal axis. The convention is:

• $\hat{i}$ (x-axis) → East
• $\hat{j}$ (y-axis) → North
• $\hat{k}$ (z-axis) → Vertically Up

The Key Insight: Gravity only acts along the $\hat{k}$ direction (downward). The $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ components experience zero acceleration. So:

• $x(t) = u_x t$ (constant velocity East)
• $y(t) = u_y t$ (constant velocity North)
• $z(t) = u_z at - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$ (parabolic arc upward)

4. ccelerated River Crossings — When the Boat Starts from Rest

Concept: River Crossing Strategies (Complete Overview)

river flows with velocity $v_R$ (horizontal). boat can travel at speed $v_{BR}$ relative to the water. There are three key strategies:

Strategy 1 — Shortest Time: Point the boat straight across (perpendicular to the bank). Time $= \frac{d}{v_{BR}}$. The boat drifts downstream by $x = v_R \cdot at = \frac{d \cdot v_R}{v_{BR}}$.

Strategy 2 — Shortest Path (Zero Drift): im upstream at angle $ \theta$ where $\sin \theta = \frac{v_R}{v_{BR}}$ (only possible if $v_{BR} > v_R$). The effective crossing speed is $\sqrt{v_{BR}^2 - v_R^2}$. Time $= \frac{d}{\sqrt{v_{BR}^2 - v_R^2}}$.

Strategy 3 — ccelerated Crossing (NEW!): What if the boat doesn't have constant velocity but starts from rest and accelerates? This creates a fundamentally different path — a parabolic trajectory instead of a straight line.

Concept: The $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ Equation (Prerequisite)

When a body accelerates uniformly from initial velocity $u$ to final velocity $v$ over displacement $s$:

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

This is derived by eliminating time from $v = u + at$ and $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$. It's incredibly powerful because it connects velocity directly to displacement without needing time.

For the boat starting from rest ($u = 0$) with acceleration $a$ perpendicular to the river:
$v_y^2 = 0 + 2ay \implies v_y = \sqrt{2ay}$
The boat's crossing speed increases as it moves — it gets faster the farther it goes!

Solving Numerical: ccelerated River Path — The Differential Equation (Pg 36)

Problem: river flows with constant velocity $v$ (horizontal). boat starts from rest and accelerates with constant acceleration $a$ straight across (perpendicular). Find the path equation $y = f(x)$.

Step 1 — Set up coordinates: Let $x$A = downstream drift (along river), $y$A = distance crossed (perpendicular).

Step 2 — Velocity in each direction:
• Downstream: $\frac{dx}{dt} = v$ (constant river flow) → ②
• cross: $v_y = \sqrt{2ay}$ (from $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ with $u = 0$), so $\frac{dy}{dt} = \sqrt{2ay}$ → ①

Step 3 — Eliminate time by dividing:
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \frac{\sqrt{2ay}}{v}$

Step 4 — Separate variables:
$\frac{v}{\sqrt{2a}} \cdot \frac{dy}{\sqrt{y}} = dx$
$\frac{v}{\sqrt{2a}} \int y^{-1/2}\, dy = \int dx$

Step 5 — Integrate both sides:
$\frac{v}{\sqrt{2a}} \cdot 2\sqrt{y} = x$
$\frac{2v\sqrt{y}}{\sqrt{2a}} = x$

Step 6 — Square both sides:
$\frac{4v^2 y}{2a} = x^2 \implies \frac{2v^2 y}{a} = x^2$
$$\boxed{y = \frac{a x^2}{2v^2}}$$

This is a parabola opening in the y-direction! Unlike constant-velocity crossing (which gives a straight line), acceleration creates a curved path. The faster the river ($v$ large), the flatter the curve (more drift). The stronger the acceleration ($a$ large), the steeper the curve (less drift).

6. Two-Projectile Collision — The Grand Derivation

Concept: When Two Projectiles Collide (from Scratch)

Imagine two cannons separated by horizontal distance $a$. Cannon 1 (at height $0$) fires at angle $\alpha$ with speed $u$. Cannon 2 (at height $h$ above cannon 1) fires at angle $\beta$ with speed $v$. When do they collide?

For collision, both projectiles must be at the same point at the same time.

Solving Numerical: Full Derivation — The $\cot\alpha - \cot\beta$ Formula (Pg 18)

Setup: Cannon 1 at origin fires at angle $\alpha$ with speed $u$. Cannon 2 at distance $a$ horizontally and height $h$ fires at angle $\beta$ with speed $v$.

Horizontal motion of projectile 1:
$x_1 = u\cos\alpha \cdot t$. For it to reach the collision point at horizontal distance $a$:
$a = u\cos\alpha \cdot at \implies at = \frac{a}{u\cos\alpha}$ → ①

Vertical motion of projectile 1:
$y_1 = u\sin\alpha \cdot at - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
So: $\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = u\sin\alpha \cdot at - y_1$. at collision height $y_1$:
$\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = h + a\tan\alpha$ → ③ (substituting $t$ from ①)

Horizontal motion of projectile 2:
From cannon 2's perspective: $a = v\cos\beta \cdot at \implies at = \frac{a}{v\cos\beta}$ → ④

Vertical motion of projectile 2:
$h - y_2 = v\sin\beta \cdot at - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
$\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = v\sin\beta \cdot at - h = a\tan\beta - h$ → ⑥

Equating ③ and ⑥ (both equal $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$):
$h + a\tan\alpha = a\tan\beta - h$
$a\tan\beta - a\tan\alpha = 2h$
$a(\tan\beta - \tan\alpha) = 2h$

Converting to cotangent: $\tan\beta - \tan\alpha = \frac{1}{\cot\beta} - \frac{1}{\cot\alpha} = \frac{\cot\alpha - \cot\beta}{\cot\alpha \cdot \cot\beta}$

$$\boxed{a(\tan\beta - \tan\alpha) = 2h}$$

This is the collision condition — it connects the geometry (height $h$, distance $a$) to the launch angles ($\alpha, \beta$). If the angles don't satisfy this equation, the projectiles will never meet!

speed-time graph plots speed ($v$) on the y-axis and time ($t$) on the x-axis. The area under the curve gives the total distance travelled.

• Triangle (uniformly accelerating from 0): Area = $\frac{1}{2} imes \text{base} imes \text{height} = \frac{1}{2}vt$

From Image 2 (Top Left): Particle 1 is launched from $O'$ (height $2h$) upwards with speed $u$ at angle $\alpha$. Particle 2 is launched from $O$ (ground level) with speed $V$ at angle $\beta$. They collide at mid-air point $P$ at horizontal distance $a$ and height $h$. Prove that $a(\tan\beta - \tan\alpha) = 2h$.

Step 1 - Horizontal Equivalence:
Since they collide at distance $a$, they must have the same horizontal time equation:
$t = \frac{a}{u\cos\alpha} = \frac{a}{V\cos\beta}$

Step 2 - Vertical Displacement for Particle 1 (Top down to P):
It starts at $2h$ and lands at $h$, so displacement is $-h$. Since it's thrown upwards at angle $\alpha$:
$-h = u\sin\alpha at - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies \frac{1}{2}gt^2 = h + u\sin\alpha t$
Substitute $t = \frac{a}{u\cos\alpha}$:
$\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = h + u\sin\alpha \left(\frac{a}{u\cos\alpha}\right) \implies \mathbf{\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = h + a\tan\alpha}$

Step 3 - Vertical Displacement for Particle 2 (Ground up to P):
It starts at $0$ and lands at $h$, so displacement is $h$.
$h = V\sin\beta at - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies \frac{1}{2}gt^2 = V\sin\beta at - h$
Substitute $t = \frac{a}{V\cos\beta}$:
$\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = V\sin\beta \left(\frac{a}{V\cos\beta}\right) - h \implies \mathbf{\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = a\tan\beta - h}$

Step 4 - Equating the Gravity:
Set the two $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$ equations equal to each other:
$h + a\tan\alpha = a\tan\beta - h$
$2h = a\tan\beta - a\tan\alpha \implies \mathbf{a(\tan\beta - \tan\alpha) = 2h}$

Chapter 5.5: Absolute Mastery (Radius of Curvature)

You have reached the pinnacle of kinematics. At any single microscopic instant, a curve is effectively a piece of a perfect circle. But how big is that circle?

In circular motion, the centripetal acceleration ($a_A\perp$) needed to stay in a circle of radius $R$ at velocity $v$ is $a_A\perp = \frac{v^2}{R}$. We can rearrange this to find the Radius of Curvature for any path!

$$ R = \frac{v^2}{a_A\perp} $$

Where $a_A\perp$ is the component of gravity that is perpendicular to the velocity vector.

  • At Launch: Velocity $v$ is max ($u$), but gravity $g$ is pointing mostly straight down, so only $g\cos \theta$ is perpendicular. The circle is HUGE.
  • At the Peak: Velocity is at its absolute minimum ($u\cos \theta$), and gravity is perfectly perpendicular to the flat velocity vector ($a_A\perp = g$). The circle is at its SMALLEST!
